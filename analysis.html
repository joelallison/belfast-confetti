ANNOTATION

Key Definitions:

  • Fount: a spring or fountain; or, variant spelling of font [line 3]
  • Type: characters or letters that are printed or shown on a screen [line 3]
  • Balaclava: Battle in 1854 during the Crimean War; or, headgear that covers most of the face [line 10]
  • Raglan: Commander in Battle of Balaclava [line 10]
  • Inkerman: Battle in 1854 during the Crimean War [line 10]
  • Odessa: Siege during WW2 [line 11]
  • Crimea: Autonomous republic of Ukraine [line 12]
  • Saracen(s): (Medieval) a people distinguishable from those known as Arabs. By 12th Century synonymous with ‘Muslim’; or, 6-wheeled armoured truck used during the Troubles [line 14]
  • Kremlin-2 mesh: protective metal grate for armoured trucks [line 14]
  • Makrolon face-shields: face guard for riot police [line 14]
  • Fusillade: a round of bullets fired by several guns at once [line 16]

In the second stanza the street names as used as metaphors and named after generals, battles and places from the Crimea war which is saying the riot is a war.
In the first stanza it says “Nuts, bolts, nails, car-keys” this is what the rioters have to riot with,
but in the second stanza it says “a Saracen, Kremlin-2 mesh. Makrolon face-shields. Walkie-talkies.”
This is what the policeman has to stop the rioters.

Each line spills over into the next this is to express the confusion found in the riot.
The lines are long and stretched.
The poem starts with exclamation marks and ends with question marks,
this means the poem starts with answers and ends with the questions.
The poem is back to front and is inverted structure.
